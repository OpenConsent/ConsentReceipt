Abstract

This specification provides a structure for an open, standardized format to create a consent record. Records of consent, when available, do not share a common format, and are rarely viewable by the end users; such records are considered 'closed'. Current legal guidelines, however, require that consent records be more open and clear to the end user.. Closed consent records inhibit innovation and the development of trust frameworks and personal data control. As a result, the Consent & Information Sharing Working Group (CISWG) within the Kantara Initiative is distilling a small, common set of consent requirements for information sharing that are salient across jurisdictions with Fair Information Practices based privacy instruments and standards.[i] These common requirements constitute the Minimum Viable Consent Recipet, specifying the minimum of information required including expected links, fields and data formats to meet the most basic of obligations of notice for consent. This specification is structured so that purpose specification and personal data collection can be listed and used to specify information sharing and to generate consent.

These minimum consent requirements are common across jurisdictions and are assessed in their capacity to address the requirements of the Privacy Principle of Openness, Transparency and Notification in order to facilitate a greater capacity for Consent and Choice.

1       INTRODUCTION

The Minimum Viable Consent Receipt (MVCR) aims to specify the common consent elements that are salient across legal jurisdictions and contexts. The term 'Minimum' in the MVCR refers to the least amount of fields required to make a consent receipt viable. Viable, in this context, means to use the consent receipt independently of the issuer. The consent receipt MUST contain the minimal notice requirements for an individual to inquire with the company or organization issuing the receipt in a fair and reasonable manner.[1]

The fields in this v0.7 specification are the Alpha MVCR fields that the CISWG will use to implement and test the consent receipt.

2       Problem Statement(s)

The MVCR seeks to solve for a variety of problems that exist in the online marketplace today. Information sharing is a complex issue for organizations that require consent for the collection of personal information from individuals and have specific legal obligations related to the context and the collection of personal data. These obligations require privacy policies and/or notices about how personal information may be collected, used, disclosed, retained and disposed of or deleted. As each organization posts their policies in different locations, and often change the content and URI of the policies, this is systematically unusable. Each organization has different industry practices, policy structures and policy formats. This results in a lack of transparency that violates the Openness (transparency and notice) Privacy Principles, is very costly to manage for all stakeholders, unusable for people, and very difficult to regulate effectively.

Consent is Closed

There is no common consent record format; people cannot see what they have agreed to in any given transaction. Each organization has their own unique privacy policy, policy structures, publishing practices and policy formats, again making it difficult for a person to know—let alone track—what they have consented to. Closed policies and opaque consent information violates the Openness Privacy Principles found in all privacy instruments: FIPPs, APEC, Canadian, European and US Law. [ii]

Consent is Not Scalable

As described above, there is no common consent record format and no standard practice to bind trust frameworks and log permission related to a single consent format.

This is compounded by each organization posting their policies in different locations, using different trust frameworks, different technology and using different multi party profiling and tracking practices.

Cost, Regulation & Usability

Consent management is very costly to manage at scale, and is both difficult to regulate and unusuable for the typical consumer. The scale of information sharing however, continues to grow at a phenomenal rate, with 90% of all data in the world being generated in the last two years. [2]

In order to simplify management, manage costs, and provide value to the consumer as intended by the creation of the Openness Privacy Principles, a common format for consent information is necessary. A common format will allow easier auditing and reporting by and to regulators to demonstrate adherence with the privacy principles and local legal requirements. It will allow for more standardized tools for reporting and a more "out of the box" utility for organizations that will no longer need to create and support a unique consent management environment. Finally, it will allow a consumer more control over their own information and how it is shared.

 

 

3       Solution: A CONSENT Receipt

The solution to managing consent in a scalable and cost-effective manner is a common way to record consent and to provide an 'open consent' model that is based upon a simple, standardized, easy-to-read consent record. The act of providing a consent record itself demonstrates the capacity for compliance, lessens liability and builds trust in the management of personal data. The use of contact information for inquiry demonstrates compliance with privacy law.

A common consent receipt format will make it easier for industry to generate and will enable people to communicate about specific consent requirements after the point at which consent is provided.

This transparency is iterative and makes consent more usable as it provides the means for people and CRM systems to evolve towards greater personal data control in line with regulation and increasing demand for granular and usable consent and access controls.

Consent Buttons can record each preference, incorporate Information Sharing Labels[3], so that people can see what they are going to consent to prior to clicking to consent and provide a receipt (that is independently usable to all parties post consent).

4       Scope

CISWG - Internal Specification revision: This iteration of the MVCR specification is the alpha functional version.

Core use is to communicate fine-grained consent contexts, using existing policy, between DS and DC.

The Minimum Viable Consent Receipt (MVCR) specifies a set of fields required for creating a legitimate digital consent record.

The scope of the MVCR is limited to defining the minimum consent fields for an explicit consent, with the ultimate objective of proposing a wider set of fields that capture additional information and will simply be called a consent receipt (CR).

This format is agnostic to jurisdiction or specific industry requirements; it is intended to be a lateral specification that can be used to record any type of digital consent.

5       MVCR: Core Profile

Design Principles (TBD v0.8)

  The MVCR is designed so that the purposes and the data specified in consent can be re-used to specify information sharing policy with first, second, and third parties.

  The first consideration is usability from a human centric perspective. The MVCR must be:

o     Human Readable - read like a receipt, usable at a glance

o     Machine Readable

o     Offer a standard, mandatory structure and common field definitions

  Structured by logic function

  Easily extended

  Labeling and Terminology of fields must be consistent

  Context & Method of Collection is recognized to have an impact on design considerations

  Consider Accessibility guidelines: ISO/IEC 40500, Information technology -- W3C Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0

  Take into account various legal considerations.

Functional Objective

The MVCR is structured into five extendable (beyond the MVCR) sections designed for usability. The five sections are:

1.     Header

2.     Data Controller Contact & Policy

3.     Purpose Specification

4.     Personal Information

5.     Information Sharing

When an organization seeks consent from PI Principals, they shall display each item in the order specified. Each item should be displayed as a headline in a table format for corresponding values. However, in the absence of corresponding value, the item can be omitted. If the screen is too small to fit all the relevant information in a table format, a text format can be used. However, the order of appearance and order of packaging the format SHALL NOT be changed.

Displaying items in a fixed order in a table format makes the comparison of different notice easier, helping PII principals to form aggregate decisions.

Receipt Header

The administrative data for a consent receipt, which MUST include; the jurisdiction, time and date stamp, location of processing (i.e., domain name), unique consent record id, and receiving parties point of contact.

Data Controller Contact & Privacy Policy

The consent receipt MUST contain name or title and the contact information of DC, if this is a delegated DC, this information primary purpose is for preference communication in the context of the privacy policy. Contact MUST includes the address and direct contact information for consent management. The quality and service level of the contact information can vary dramatically.

Purpose Specification

The purpose MUST be explicit, specified and legitimate.
The purpose MUST contain the service name to be explicit and the action requiring this purpose, it MAY contain an external reference to an on and off preference, for this purpose. Finally, a purpose specification SHOULD contain additional options, for example, link to full purpose description, data retention period.

Specifying the service name and the purpose for using personal information is required. This can be used post consent for preference management, privileges/roles assignment, consent directives and other access authorization policy.

A Purpose list, definitions of these purposes, and guidance/best practices are being discussed with a initial list for v0.7 MVCR to be found in Appendix A.

Personal Information Specification (PI Collected)

Personal Information that is collected for the purposes specified are split into two types: A) Categories of PI which MUST be included and PI Attributes that SHOULD include the data captured at the point of consent.[4]

B) Sensitive PI (a category of PI, with a yes/no indicator) defined by the creator of the consent receipt button.

There is a distinction in legislation between sensitive and non-sensitive personal information. This is intended to flag that additional notice and consent considerations are required. [5]

In the MVCR, if a Sensitive PI category is selected then additional notice and purpose requirements exist, these are beyond the scope of the MVCR.

For example: A common Sensitive category list would be data relating to:

Personal Health [X]

Child PI [ ]

Financial [ ]

Sexual Life [ ]

and would have an ability to add more categories.

In this example if the data capture was health related in the US, then health should be displayed and a HIPAA compliant purpose list and Health PI category list added to the receipt.

Information Sharing Specification

A sharing relationship can be specified in the consent receipt and this provides structure for organization to provide required notice of sharing with Third Parties in the consent record. The sharing specification is used to capture the purpose and attributes used for sharing. [6]

Creating a sharing specification: The MVCR is designed so that the purposes and the data specified in consent can be re-used to specify sharing with Third Parties (see Design Principles in Section 5).

Consent Receipt Generation

There are numerous methods identified for creating consent receipts[7]. The work group at this time welcomes use cases, which are currently categorized by three types depending on how many parties are involved. (See Appendix D)

One Party Use Case:

A One Party consent receipt is when the PI Principle, or an auditor, creates his or her own record independently.

This is represented by Consent Receipt Request Use Case captured CIS Kantara Wiki.

The consent receipt request form that scrapes the consent transaction to create a record and creates a requests for the status of the consent, which is generated using the information scraped from the point in time the consent was provided.

Two Party Use Case

Two Party is generating a consent receipt where there is only the consent receipt provider and receiver. A two party example for a general website use case can be found here: https://kantarainitiative.org/confluence/x/ToVtB. This is expected to be the predominant way to provide consent receipts.[8]

Three (or Multi-Party) Use Cases

In this context a consent receipt would be provided using a Third Party. This category of use cases encompasses, third party agent, shared and or delegated data resources/controller use cases.

There is an active 3rd Party Consent Receipt Generator Demo as a part of developing this version of the specification, which can be found at http://api.consentreceipt.org/

The demo is usable and demonstrates the API, which is usable to create a consent 2.0 Alpha Receipt button.[9] On this page there is a form that uses the API to render and make downloadable as JWT a Consent Receipt in the web page.

This is an example of a third party issuance of a consent receipt and is required for exploring different legal and liability considerations in consent management.

To do this we have built a test generator, which is an example site that consists of two web pages. The first for the Consent Receipt Generator (CRG) which is used for providing consent field input and showing the resultant receipt rendering. The second page is for documentation. This is used to help develop implementations and see how the consent receipt (GitHub repository) code is working.

Terms and definitions

For the purposes of this document, the terms and definitions given in ISO/IEC 29100 and the following apply.

Purpose Specification - [specified in Article 29 WP Paper ] The purpose of the collection must be clearly and specifically identified: it must be detailed enough to determine what kind of processing is and is not included within the specified purpose, and to allow that compliance with the law can be assessed and data protection safeguards applied.

Key to consent is the 'purpose specification; and needs to be specific, explicit and legitimate consent. 

Personal Information - all attributes that are collected about the individual and the consent session can be deemed personal.

Information Sharing - [definition needed]

Sensitive Personal Information - Categories - refer to the legal categories defined in some jurisdictions like the EU and the US, which have additional notice and consent requirements and require explicit consent.

Individual - this is referred to in this document and maps to existing terms, Data Subject, the PI Principal in ISO 29100 and is commonly know as a person.

Low Risk by Default - This is the expression used for organizations to self assert that their data process and data use practices are very low risk with a minimum viable consent receipt meeting requirements of small and medium enterprise with basic data protection requirements. In the MVCR if No to sensitive personal data and no to information sharing then this indicates a self asserted low risk high compliance, and a default set to express (Trusted) use of consent. This is a customer friendly approach for market level consent management. A checklist of implementation requirements should accompany this. (TBD) guiding. [11]

Appendix C: Categories of Personal Data

Appended here is a basic list of Personal Data Categories for implementations to play with for creating consent receipts.

Sensitive Data Categories

Some laws make distinctions on what is sensitive data. Sensitive data is required by all laws to require explicit consent. These will have defined specifications and notice requirements in each jurisdiction and required third party frameworks for multi-jurisdictional use.

If any attributes taken in these categories or in even in this context, these can be deemed sensitive data categories, then Sensitive Data Sharing Must be indicated in order for the Principle to understand what data protections and privacy protections should be in place.

These Categories Include:

[Health]
[Financial]
[Child Data]
[Location]
[Official Identifiers]
[Psycho/Social/Belief] – Sexual Behavior, Religion,
[Membership] – Union or political party
[Other] – Include Sensitive Context

Sensitive Personal Data Attributes can change from jurisdiction to jurisdiction.
